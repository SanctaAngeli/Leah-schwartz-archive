# The Book Is the Score — a book designer's concepts for the Leah Schwartz site

*Reviewer lens: editorial typography and book design. I read the 1990 Strawberry Press scan page by page as a designed object. The website should not "feature" this book; it should converse with it — adopt its system where the system is good (it mostly is), and do on screen only the things the book physically could not.*

---

## 1. What I saw in the book

**The jacket (p.1).** A wraparound: irises and red tulips bleed across the front, a Dijon market scene on the back, her name twice — once in big serif caps, once as her actual looping signature printed beside the flap copy. The book announces from the cloth out that the author, painter, and publisher are the same person. Price on the flap: $50.00. She priced her own life.

**The title page (p.6).** This is the best page in the book and any website should be built from it. No typeset author name at all — her signature, enlarged to perhaps 20× writing size and screened back to a pale silver-grey, sweeps across the whole recto. The subtitle, *the life of a woman who managed to keep painting*, rides the baseline of the signature in italic ITC Galliard, tucked under the descender of the "Schwartz" Z like a passenger. Identity rendered as gesture, title rendered as type, the two interlocked. That's the site's logotype, ready-made; designing a new one would be vandalism.

**The colophon (p.7).** "Designed by Noreen Poli… Digitally set in ITC Galliard by Ron LaFon and MMT, Santa Rosa, CA… Strawberry Press logo by Leah Schwartz. Printed by Graphic Resources International, Singapore." This is 1990 desktop publishing at its most earnest — digital Galliard, a botanical woodcut press device *she drew herself* (p.5: a spiny caudiciform succulent, perfectly on brand for her husband's botanical press at 227 Strawberry Drive). The book is self-published but not amateur: it has a designer, a consultant, a typesetter, an ISBN. The "self" in self-published here means *self-determined*, not unedited.

**The dedication (p.8).** Top right corner of an otherwise empty page: `D E D I C A T E D` in letterspaced caps, then two italic lines — "to my dear husband, / the remarkable Herman Schwartz." The whole front matter trusts white space at a level most trade publishers would never allow. Pages 2–5 and 9 are nearly empty. The book *breathes before it speaks*.

**The contents (p.10).** A persimmons still life faces a single letterspaced-caps column: AUTOBIOGRAPHY, OLD STUFF, ABSTRACT, SOCIAL COMMENT, ON THE ROAD, LANDSCAPE, STREET SCENES, PORTRAITS, STILL LIFE, INTERIORS, FLOWERS, TRAVEL, LIST OF PAINTINGS, INDEX. Twelve chapters. Note the names: not "Early Work" but **OLD STUFF**. Not "Political Paintings" but **SOCIAL COMMENT**. The taxonomy is hers, colloquial, and must be preserved verbatim.

**The chapter system (pp.27, 127, and throughout).** Identical every time, and it's a real system:
- Chapter name: bold Galliard caps, flush left on a column that starts at the page's horizontal midline (e.g., "OLD STUFF," "PORTRAITS")
- Beneath it, an italic lowercase tagline, the chapter's whole thesis in 3–6 words: *salvage of thirty years*; *beauty and self-possession*; (autobiography's first section) *genetic baggage*
- Then a first-person essay in a single narrow column (~38–40 characters? no — roughly 65 chars; measure ≈ 4.1″), ragged right, indented paragraphs, no display initials, no rules
- The left half of the page stays **empty**. Half a page of silence before every movement.

**The plate pages and the caption voice.** Paintings sit on white with generous margins, caption set small in a humanist sans (Gill-ish caps), bottom-right, ragged toward the image edge. The caption grammar is rigid and idiosyncratic at once:

> `SELF PORTRAIT, 1949-50 tempera, 14x18` (p.29)
> `THE SENATOR (on an investigating committee) oil, 18x18` (p.47)
> `THE MEADOWS, approx. 16x20 (collection of D.& M.Forbes). This was The Last Roadhouse in our area, maybe the last anywhere. It has vanished.` (p.67)
> `MY FIRST ART SCHOOL PAINTING (just like the teacher's)` (p.24)
> `ME, (pregnant with Daniel), WITH SISTER-IN-LAW, TAMARA` (p.24)
> `UCHISAR, CAPPODOCIA, 5x8. Awoke at 5a.m. to the sound of the muezzin calling from the village across the canyon. He was answered by a cuckoo on our side.` (p.257)

Title in caps; the parenthetical aside in lowercase is where her voice leaks into the apparatus; sometimes the caption keeps going and becomes a diary entry. Some plates (the pears, p.179/book 192) carry **no caption at all** — identification deferred to the back matter. The caption is not metadata here. It is *prose wearing metadata's clothes.*

**Scale is honest.** A 5×8 watercolor (MT. TAM FROM SONOMA, p.87) is reproduced *small*, floating high on an empty page. AN AMERICAN FAMILY, oil, 40×54, fills its page edge to edge (p.47). The reproductions are roughly proportional to the real objects. Almost no art book does this, and almost no art website does. She did.

**The travel section (~pp.223–287 of the book).** Prose turns into a run-in diary — "GREECE" as a bold run-in head, then torrential first-person reportage with onomatopoeic ALL CAPS ("BLASTING, BLARING, BOOMING!"), Jolly Cola through thin straws, Danish artists stamping paint with their running shoes. Small watercolors carry long site-specific captions. Crucially, several painted sheets still show **her pencil inscriptions on the paper itself** ("BRITTANY" handwritten under the wash, p.225/book 238). The artifact carries two text layers: typeset Galliard and graphite autograph.

**The back matter — read it; it's the soul.** The LIST OF PAINTINGS (p.289) is organized *by chapter*, with title/medium/size/page — her own catalogue raisonné, in sans-serif columns. And then the INDEX (pp.292–) — the most revealing pages in the book. It is a **single alphabet of an entire life**: paintings in italic, people in roman ("Abozo, U.R. (New York landlord) 16"), places (Acapulco, Ankara, Arezzo, Assisi), and *subjects she decided her life contained*: "Art School, Love, Marriage 10." "Botanizing in Kenya 269." "Cream of Tartar 158." "Death of Parents 23." "Bicycle Women 112." No professional indexer writes "Cream of Tartar" into an artist monograph. This index is an autobiography in alphabetical order — flat, democratic, funny, and devastating, with a landlord and a senator and a pot of flowers given equal typographic citizenship.

**The book's design system, named:**
1. *Half-page silence* — text columns occupy the right half; white space is structural, not residual
2. *Galliard for the voice, small sans caps for the apparatus* — two-font hierarchy, never violated
3. *CAPS title + (lowercase parenthetical aside)* — the caption grammar
4. *Chapter = NAME IN CAPS + italic tagline + narrow essay column*
5. *Honest scale* — reproduction size tracks object size
6. *Her hand as a display face* — signature as logotype, pencil titles on the sheets
7. *Sequence over symmetry* — plates are paced (a tiny watercolor page is a rest; a 40×54 oil is a downbeat), and some plates go captionless because the back matter holds the record
8. *The index as the truest self-portrait*

Everything below is built from these eight observations and nothing else.

## 2. The concepts

### Concept A — ONE ALPHABET
*The index is the museum. I believe this has never been built on the web.*

**Core idea.** The site's primary navigation is Leah's own index, digitized and made total: 88 people, 68 places, 99 subjects, 267 paintings, one merged alphabet, every entry live. You do not enter through a hero carousel; you enter through "A — Abozo, U.R. (New York landlord)" and you leave hours later somewhere around "Cream of Tartar."

**Bookness preserved / transformed.** Preserves: her exact entries, her exact typographic distinction (paintings italic, people roman, subjects roman), the flat democracy of one alphabet — landlord beside senator beside still life. Transforms: a print index points one way (entry → page); the web makes it bidirectional and dense. Every entry resolves not to a number but to *the actual material* — the scanned book page, the painting at full resolution, the paragraph of her prose where the cream of tartar appears, the geocoded place. And every painting page lists, in index style, the entries that point to it: a **reverse index**, which print cannot do.

**Typographic/layout specifics.**
- The index screen IS the homepage below the signature masthead: a multi-column alphabet (4 columns ≥1280px, 2 at tablet, 1 mobile), set in a Galliard-class face (Galliard is licensable; fallback: Bell or Caslon with strong italics). Entries 15–16px/1.5, hanging indent 1.5em for runovers exactly as printed.
- Page numbers stay — set as old-style figures, and they are the links. Hovering "Cream of Tartar **158**" lifts a thumbnail of book page 158 (the real scan) like a slip of paper; click opens it with the matching passage located.
- Letter dividers as letterspaced small caps with a hairline above — the only rules on the page.
- A single filter row, styled like the index's own typography, no pills, no chips: *paintings · people · places · subjects · everything* — dimming (not removing) the excluded classes, so the alphabet never reflows. The index is a fixed text; the reader's attention is the only thing that filters.
- Painting entries open the plate view (Concept B's viewer). People entries open every page they appear on plus every photo caption naming them. Place entries open the map-pinned travel diary entry plus the paintings made there.
- The URL scheme honors it: `/index/c#cream-of-tartar`. Shareable index slips.

**Hero moment.** First visit: the alphabet builds itself letter by letter (fast, 1.5s total, type appearing in reading order), then a single entry highlights at random and a small italic line appears under the masthead: *"An autobiography in alphabetical order."* Reload, different entry. The randomizer is the front door — one click and you're on page 158 learning why cream of tartar mattered.

**Data used.** Digitized index (255 entries, classed + geocoded), page-number→scan-file mapping, painting catalog, prose corpus with page anchors, photo captions.

**Difficulty note.** The hard part is editorial, not technical: every index entry must be verified against its target page (her page numbers occasionally drift, as in any 1990 self-published book), and prose anchors require locating each reference in the OCR'd text. Budget a full verification pass of all 255 entries. The build itself is a weekend; the fidelity is the labor. Honest risk: visitors who don't read will bounce off a wall of text — mitigate with the hover-thumbnails, which turn the alphabet into a field of latent images.

### Concept B — ACTUAL SIZE
*The first art site where the paintings are as big as the paintings. No bigger.*

**Core idea.** The book reproduced her work roughly proportional to its true dimensions — a 5×8 Mt. Tam sits small on a sea of white; a 40×54 oil fills the sheet. The site takes this to its logical, physical conclusion: after a one-time screen calibration, every painting displays at **true 1:1 physical scale**, and the layout, like the book's, gives small works enormous margins instead of inflating them.

**Bookness preserved / transformed.** Preserves: honest scale, white as structure, the caption grammar verbatim (CAPS title, year, medium, dimensions, parenthetical aside, set small in humanist sans at bottom-right of the image, ragged toward it). Transforms: the book could only *approximate* scale within a 9×11 trim; the screen, calibrated, can be exact — and for the oils larger than any screen, it inverts honestly: you see a viewport-sized *detail at 1:1* and a small diagram showing how little of the painting fits on your machine. The limitation becomes the exhibit.

**Typographic/layout specifics.**
- Calibration: a drawn credit card / driver's license outline the visitor pinches or drags to match a real card against the glass; store device px-per-inch in localStorage. Skippable (falls back to assumed 96–110 ppi with an italic disclaimer in caption style: *(approximate — calibrate for actual size)*).
- Plate view: painting centered on #FAFAFA, margins computed as in the book — minimum 18% of viewport on all sides for works that fit; the 5×8s will float tiny and high, exactly like book page 87/100, and that smallness is the point. No zoom chrome on works that fit; the scan IS the size.
- Oversize works (anything exceeding viewport at 1:1): open on a 1:1 crop centered on the painting's focal area; a thumbnail minimap bottom-left with a viewport rectangle; drag to pan across the surface like walking past it. A toggle set as a caption line: *shown actual size — see it whole (reduced)*.
- Caption block: 11–12px humanist sans caps (Gill Sans Nova, or Alegreya Sans SC), asides in lowercase parentheses, two-line max width equal to the image, right-aligned under the image's right edge — pixel-faithful to the book.
- Sequence: prev/next follows **book plate order within her chapters**, with chapter half-titles (NAME + italic tagline on an empty screen) appearing between sections as palate-cleansers — the half-page silence, translated.
- A small constant footer line in old-style figures: `40 × 54 in · shown at 100%` — the only UI text on the page.

**Hero moment.** AN AMERICAN FAMILY (oil, 40×54). The screen fills with a 1:1 detail — the mother's face, the bandolier — and the minimap shows your 13-inch laptop as a small rectangle lost inside a four-and-a-half-foot canvas. One line fades in, caption-style: `AN AMERICAN FAMILY oil, 40x54 — your screen is showing 11% of it.` Nobody who sees that forgets that she painted big.

**Data used.** Real dimensions for all 267 paintings (already in catalog), 300 DPI crops (300 DPI comfortably exceeds 1:1 on every consumer display), chapter/sequence order, caption text verbatim.

**Difficulty note.** Calibration UX must be genuinely one-touch or people will skip it; test the no-calibration fallback hard. 300 DPI sources are ample (a 1:1 display needs only ~110 ppi) but the oversize oils produce 100+ MB source images — requires tiled deep-zoom serving (IIIF or OpenSeadragon-style pyramids). Medium build complexity, high payoff. The real risk is curatorial discipline: the temptation to "let people zoom in further" on small works must be resisted on the main view (offer a separate *examine the surface* mode) or the scale argument collapses.

### Concept C — THE SECOND SETTING
*Not a scanned book, not a flipbook: a new typesetting of the whole text, with the 1990 edition running underneath it like a facsimile en face.*

**Core idea.** Re-set all 28,660 of her words as one continuous, beautifully measured vertical text — her twelve chapters, her section heads, her taglines, her captions — in live type, with the plates entering the stream at their book positions; and at any point, one keystroke (or a persistent marginal page number) flips you to the actual scanned 1990 page you are "on." A critical edition: new setting recto, facsimile verso, except the fold is the z-axis.

**Bookness preserved / transformed.** Preserves: the entire reading order — front matter, blanks-as-pauses, chapter half-titles, plate pacing, back matter; the two-font system; the narrow measure; the half-page silence (text column lives right-of-center on wide screens, left half reserved for marginalia). Transforms: page breaks become scroll rhythm; the book's *physical* page numbers become a live margin apparatus — small old-style folios floating in the empty left half, always telling you where you'd be in the printed object, each one clickable to summon the scan. Rejecting the flipbook explicitly: page-turning is a storage mechanism, not a reading experience; what's worth keeping is sequence, measure, and pause, all of which scroll can carry.

**Typographic/layout specifics.**
- Measure: 62–68 characters, ~4.1″ equivalent (max-width ~34rem at 19px), positioned starting at the horizontal midline on ≥1024px viewports; full-width centered on mobile. Galliard-class serif, 19px/1.58, paragraph indents (1.5em) not spaces — exactly as printed.
- Chapter openings reproduce the system at screen scale: viewport-height "page" with NAME in bold caps (clamp 28–40px, letterspaced +2%), italic tagline beneath at text size, then the essay begins. Generous: the opening screen is mostly empty, like p.127.
- Plates: photos and paintings enter at their printed positions, sized by the book's own proportions (a small watercolor renders ~38% column width; full-page plates interrupt the column and take the viewport). Captions in the sans caps grammar. Photos keep her first-person captions: `ME, (pregnant with Daniel), WITH SISTER-IN-LAW, TAMARA`.
- The facsimile layer: press `f` or click the marginal folio — the scanned spread fades up full-screen (the actual 300 DPI page, paper tone and all), scroll-locked to the same passage; `f` again returns. Reading position is bidirectionally synced via a paragraph→page concordance.
- Travel diary entries get their dated run-in heads (`GREECE` style: bold caps run-in, no extra leading) and her ALL-CAPS outbursts are preserved letter-for-letter — no copyediting her voice, ever.
- Marginalia channel (the empty left half): folio numbers; tiny index-entry markers (from Concept A's data) showing which of her index entries point at the passage you're reading — e.g., reading about art school, the margin quietly shows *Art School, Love, Marriage 10* in 12px italic. Her own index, annotating her own text, live.
- Back matter is set too: LIST OF PAINTINGS as linked columns; the INDEX as the gateway to Concept A.
- Reading position persists; a thin progress hairline at top maps scroll to the book's 309 pages.

**Hero moment.** You're deep in the GREECE entry — Jolly Cola, the bouzouki, the BLASTING loudspeakers — you tap the folio `237`, and the screen becomes the physical page: same words, now in printed Galliard on slightly toned paper, with a small watercolor you hadn't scrolled to yet visible on the facing page. The artifact and the edition verify each other. Trust is the hero moment.

**Data used.** Full structured prose, paragraph→book-page concordance, all plates with positions, 300 DPI page scans, photo captions, index anchors.

**Difficulty note.** The concordance (every paragraph mapped to its printed page) is meticulous but mechanical — OCR alignment gets 90%, a human pass finishes it. Web Galliard licensing needs resolving early (Monotype) or a committed fallback chosen and never revisited. Biggest design risk: the margin apparatus creeping into clutter — the left half must stay ≥80% empty at all times, enforced as a layout constant, or the book's silence dies.

### Concept D — STRAWBERRY PRESS, 227 STRAWBERRY DRIVE
*A small museum of the object: the self-published book as a thing she made, down to the pencil under the paint.*

**Core idea.** One tightly-curated exhibit (8–12 screens, linear) about the book as artifact and act: the wraparound jacket unfolded flat; the $50.00 flap; the signature title page; her hand-drawn press device; the colophon's cast of helpers; the pencil inscriptions still visible on the travel sheets; the dedication to Herman, whose botanical press made the whole thing possible. The argument, made entirely with scans: *self-publishing in 1990 was not vanity — for a 70-year-old woman painter, it was the only institution that would have her, so she built it.*

**Bookness preserved / transformed.** Preserves: the artifact itself, at material-fetish resolution — paper tone, ink density, the silver-grey screen of the signature. Transforms: the parts of a book a reader never sees *as design* (jacket flat, colophon, press device, price) become the exhibits; deep zoom does what no reading copy allows — putting your nose against the sheet. This is where the skeuomorphism budget is spent, and spent defensibly: not fake page-turns, but *real paper at real resolution*.

**Typographic/layout specifics.**
- Linear scroll of full-bleed scan details alternating with short interpretive text blocks — and the interpretation is set in the apparatus voice: small sans caps headers + serif text, ≤80 words per block, captions never paraphrasing her words, always quoting (`"Strawberry Press logo by Leah Schwartz."` — the colophon line IS the wall label).
- Exhibit 1: the jacket scan (leah-001) presented as the flat printed sheet it was, slowly traversed edge to edge — back flap, spine, front — with the fold-lines marked by hairlines and labels (*back panel · flap · spine · front panel*).
- Exhibit 4: the title-page signature with a deep-zoom loupe; alongside it, the jacket-flap signature and the painted signatures on the watercolor sheets — three instances of her hand, same gesture, three media.
- Exhibit 7: the travel sheets where graphite shows through wash ("BRITTANY," p.238 scan) — loupe interaction, with a caption noting only what is visibly true.
- Exhibit 9: the colophon as a credits screen — each name (Noreen Poli, Robert Cooney, Ron LaFon) set large, with the colophon's own wording beneath. The people who helped her make it are part of the object's meaning.
- Closing screen: the press device woodcut, alone, centered, with the imprint lines as printed: *Strawberry Press / Mill Valley, California.*
- All text on #FAFAFA; the scans bring their own paper color — the contrast between gallery white and 35-year-old paper white is itself an exhibit; never color-correct it away.

**Hero moment.** The jacket traverse: starting on the back-flap text, gliding across the spine, arriving at the irises and her name — the visitor realizes they've just read the book the way a bookstore browser in 1990 met it, in one continuous printed sheet.

**Data used.** 300 DPI page scans (jacket, pp.5–8, 10, travel sheets, back matter), colophon text, nothing else. Smallest data footprint of the four.

**Difficulty note.** Easiest build (deep-zoom tiles + scroll choreography), hardest writing. Every interpretive sentence risks violating the no-invented-text spirit; the discipline is to caption like she did — short, concrete, slightly wry — without imitating her voice. Where context is needed (what an ISBN meant for a self-publisher, who Noreen Poli was), say it in the site's own plain editorial voice, clearly distinguished typographically (sans, never her Galliard). Verify biographical claims about the press before asserting them; everything else is visibly on the paper.

---

## Recommendation

These four are one site: **C is the spine** (the text, re-set, with the facsimile beneath), **A is the front door and the nervous system** (her index linking everything), **B is the gallery behavior** (every plate honest to the inch), **D is the vestibule** (the object that contains them all). If forced to build one first, build **A** — it is the concept I am confident has never existed on the web, it is buildable from data that already exists, and it is the page where her mind, not just her work, is the exhibit. "Cream of Tartar 158" is worth more than any hero animation.
