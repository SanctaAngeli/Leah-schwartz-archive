# The Exhibition Maker's Concepts
*Leah Schwartz: the life of a woman who managed to keep painting — what the website could BE*

I design exhibitions. I think in rooms, thresholds, sightlines, and the pace of a body
moving past pictures. I spent the afternoon walking through the 1990 Strawberry Press
book page by page, the way I'd walk a gallery before a re-hang. These are my notes and
my pitches.

## 1. What I saw in the book

**This book already IS an exhibition.** Not a metaphor — a literal one. Leah Schwartz
never got the museum retrospective, so in 1990 she built one out of paper and sold it
for $50 (price printed top-right of the copyright page). The white page is her white
wall. Twelve rooms (AUTOBIOGRAPHY through TRAVEL, per the contents page facing a
persimmon still life), a catalog (LIST OF PAINTINGS, p. 289), and a registrar's index
(p. 292). Any website that treats this as "image assets plus bio text" has demolished
the building to salvage the bricks.

**The book breathes like a good show.** The dedication sits alone in the upper right
of an otherwise empty page — a full sheet of paper spent on fourteen words. Chapter
openers (OLD STUFF p. 27, LANDSCAPE p. 83, STILL LIFE p. 149, TRAVEL p. 223) are a
title, a wry subtitle, and one column of her prose floated right, with the entire left
half of the spread left white. Then the plates come one per page. This is wall text →
threshold → room, executed by someone with real pacing instincts (designer Noreen Poli,
but the sensibility is Leah's).

**Scale is honest and eloquent.** Every caption carries real dimensions. PLUGS (p. 151)
— a watercolor of an orange outlet strip — floats as a small horizontal band in an
ocean of white; the page admits the painting is little, and the white space gives it
dignity. FOUR VIEWS OF SONOMA (p. 85) hangs four 5×7s in a salon grid. The big collages
(STATE OF THE UNION — '70s, 36×48, p. 43) get the full sheet. The book never inflates a
small picture, and that restraint is exactly what museum hanging does and websites never do.

**The captions are a docent's voice, not a registrar's.** "THE MEADOWS… This was The
Last Roadhouse in our area, maybe the last anywhere. It has vanished." (p. 67).
"BATHROOM, BODRUM, 5×8. That little black pipe… you turn the handle and get the shock
of your life." (p. 255). "ST. EMILION, 5×7. I liked the contrast between the ancient
roofs and their TV antennas." (p. 227). She walks beside you through her own show,
cracking jokes. The ON THE ROAD opener (p. 63) is a manifesto about painting vanishing
places — "vanishing qualities in our era of franchise and 'up-scaling.'"

**The autobiography is a room of its own kind.** Pages 1–25: a single right-hand column
of typeset memoir, black-and-white family photographs dropped in with small-caps
captions ("MOTHER AND ME IN CHICAGO," p. 5; "LOBSTER PICNIC – SERIOUS BUSINESS ON THE
ROCKY COAST OF MAINE," p. 19). Section heads with subtitles in her register: IMMIGRANTS
*genetic baggage* (p. 3), WORK *the accidental watercolorist* (p. 25). The famous
opening (p. 1): "GET A HUSBAND (for protection), KEEP AN IMMACULATE HOUSE… and NEVER,
BUT NEVER LET YOURSELF GO" — and her promise of "no ARTSPEAK." The voice is the
strongest single material in the whole archive after the paintings themselves.

**The index (p. 292–296) is a secret masterpiece.** "Plugs 151" sits between "Plasket,
Joe 224" and "Pont Louis Phillipe 228." Paintings, friends, hotels, cities, in-laws and
Franklin D. Roosevelt are interfiled in one democratic alphabet. It is a complete map of
a life where art, people and places were never separate categories — which is the entire
thesis of the book's title.

**Where it crowds:** the travel chapter (pp. 223–288) alternates typeset diary entries
(GREECE, p. 237 — the ferry loudspeakers "BLASTING, BLARING, BOOMING!") with sketches
two-to-a-page, and it reads as a sketchbook, faster-paced, deliberately minor-key. The
book knows the difference between a gallery wall and a vitrine of notebooks. A website
must too.

## 2. The Concepts

### Concept A — ACTUAL SIZE
*(the one I'd fight for)*

**Core idea.** A gallery that shows every painting at its true physical size — calibrated
to the visitor's actual screen — and refuses, as a matter of principle, to ever show a
painting larger than Leah painted it. The first website I know of where scale is the
curatorial argument.

**Why the book demands it.** Every caption in the book states dimensions (10×14, 5×8,
22×30, 36×48 — see any plate, and the LIST OF PAINTINGS p. 289 is essentially a
dimensions table). The book's most moving page is PLUGS (p. 151): a 5×8-inch watercolor
floated tiny in a huge white field. The page's honesty about smallness IS the emotional
content — this is a woman who painted between husband, children and housekeeping
(Introduction, p. 1), and the work is sized to that life. Blowing her 5×7s up to
full-bleed hero images would be a lie about her life. The data exists: real dimensions
for all 267 paintings.

**How a visitor moves.** Threshold: a one-time, charming calibration ritual — hold a
credit card or driver's license to the screen, drag a handle to match it (stored
thereafter). The site replies: "Your screen can hold everything up to FOUR VIEWS OF
SONOMA. 241 of Leah's 267 paintings fit at actual size." Then you enter a single long
white wall — horizontal scroll, paintings hung on a true centerline at consistent
"eye height," spaced with margins proportional to the book's. Small sketches cluster
like a salon hang; the 36×48 collages force you to *pan across* them because they're
bigger than your window — you walk past them, exactly as in a room. A discreet
size-sorted rack (smallest → largest) is the only navigation.

**Hero moment.** The visitor holds their hand up to the screen against TAKAYAMA BRIDGE
(4×7, p. 247) and realizes the painting is smaller than their palm — then scrolls into
STATE OF THE UNION (p. 43) and has to travel to see its corners. They tell their friend:
"You see the paintings at the size she actually made them. The big ones don't fit on
your screen — on purpose."

**Materials.** Dimensions for all 267 works; 300-DPI scans (more than enough pixel
density for 1:1 at any screen); captions in her voice; chapter as room grouping.

**What's hard.** Calibration is friction — must be optional with a sane default
(assume ~110 PPI, show a "sizes approximate" whisper until calibrated). Phones can't
hold a 22×30 — accept pan-to-traverse and make it feel like stepping closer, never
pinch-zoom-as-toy. Resist every stakeholder who wants a full-bleed hero image; the
concept dies the day one painting is shown over scale.

### Concept B — TWELVE ROOMS AND A CORRIDOR

**Core idea.** The whole site is one continuous walking route: the book's twelve
chapters rebuilt as twelve rooms along a single line, with her chapter prose as wall
text at each threshold and the plates hung in the book's own order and rhythm. No
homepage in the conventional sense — a lobby, then you walk.

**Why the book demands it.** The book is already sequenced by a designing intelligence:
title page → near-empty dedication page → contents-with-persimmons → AUTOBIOGRAPHY
section title alone on its sheet (p. before 1) → memoir → rooms in a deliberate
dramatic order (the angry SOCIAL COMMENT collages early, pp. 41–62; the serene FLOWERS
late, p. 191; TRAVEL as the long decrescendo). Chapter openers are textbook wall text:
title, ironic subtitle ("LANDSCAPE — *vanishing verdant valleys*," p. 83; "STILL LIFE —
*in love with simple objects*," p. 149), one column of voice, then silence. The blank
half-pages are rests in the score. Scrambling this into a filterable grid would be like
shuffling the rooms of a Beyeler show into a storage corridor.

**How a visitor moves.** Vertical scroll = walking. Each room: (1) threshold — a full
white screen holding only the chapter title and subtitle, held long enough to slow your
feet; (2) wall text — her opener prose, never paraphrased; (3) the hang — plates appear
one at a time, each given the screen alone, caption arriving a beat after the image, in
the book's order including its grouped pages (the Sonoma four hang together, p. 85);
(4) a breath — one empty screen — then the next threshold. A thin progress line (the
corridor) shows twelve marks; you can jump rooms, but within a room the order is fixed.
The AUTOBIOGRAPHY room is different on purpose: denser, text-led, black-and-white,
photographs at snapshot size — so that crossing into OLD STUFF, where SELF PORTRAIT
1949–50 (p. 29) suddenly faces you in full color, lands like leaving the archive
basement and stepping into the first gallery.

**Hero moment.** That exact threshold: twenty-five pages of typeset memoir and small
grey snapshots, then one swipe and her painted face from 1949 is looking at you. People
describe a website where "you walk through it, and there's a moment where the book
turns into color."

**Materials.** Complete prose, structured by chapter; all plates with book-page order;
the 74 photographs for the memoir room; captions verbatim.

**What's hard.** Discipline. Scroll-jacking ruins it — movement must be the visitor's
own muscle, with the design controlling *spacing*, not velocity. Linear sites worry
people about findability: the answer is Concept C as the second door, not filters
bolted onto the corridor. Twelve rooms is long; allow exit-and-resume (remember where
the visitor stood, like a museum hand-stamp).

### Concept C — THE INDEX IS THE MUSEUM
*(the one I believe has never been built on the web)*

**Core idea.** Leah's own back-of-book index — 88 people, 68 places, 99 subjects,
267 paintings, one alphabet — becomes the architecture: a great wall of entries, and
every entry is a door that instantly assembles a small exhibition of every page where
that name lives. A museum that re-hangs itself on demand, using only a 1990
self-published index as the curator.

**Why the book demands it.** Pages 292–296 are unlike any catalog index I've seen:
"Plugs 151" between "Plasket, Joe" and "Pont Louis Phillipe"; "Herman" threading
through the entire book; "Kyoto Canal with Blue Truck 249" a few lines from
"Kaiser Permanente Medical Group 21." She indexed her paintings, her friends, her
hospitals and her hotels as one continuous world — the index is her own statement that
the art and the life are not separable, which is the book's title made into a data
structure. Nobody builds entrances out of indexes; she already did.

**How a visitor moves.** The entry hall is the alphabet itself: a tall, quiet,
typographically beautiful wall of all ~520 entries (set like the book's own index page,
small and dense — the density is the point: a whole life on one wall). Entries glow
faintly by kind (person / place / subject / painting) but are NOT sorted into
categories. Touch "Herman" and the wall parts: a corridor assembles, in page order, of
every painting, photograph and prose passage where Herman appears — the Acapulco
elopement story (p. 19), the lobster picnic photograph, THE REMARKABLE HERMAN ARTHUR
SCHWARTZ portrait (p. 128), the dedication. Touch "Naxos" and you get the ferry diary
entry (p. 237) hung beside the Naxos watercolors. Each assembled corridor ends with
"adjacent entries" — the index neighbors — so you exit "Herman" past "Hidden Lake" and
wander on. Visitors traverse the life sideways, the way memory actually works.

**Hero moment.** A visitor types their own first name, or touches "Plugs," and watches
a museum corridor build itself in two seconds out of a dead woman's index entries —
paintings sliding in, a paragraph of her voice docking beside them. They tell a friend:
"Every name in her index is a door, and behind each door the site hangs a little show
just for that name."

**Materials.** The digitized index (people/places/subjects with page numbers), geocoded
places, the structured prose (to excerpt the passage at the indexed page), painting
catalog with book pages, photographs. The page number is the join key for everything —
her own join key, from 1990.

**What's hard.** Index entries point at pages, not at character offsets — excerpting
the *right* passage needs the prose structured by page, and some entries will land on
a page where the mention is glancing; the corridors must tolerate one weak object
without feeling broken. The entry wall risks intimidating mobile users — needs a
"pull one thread" affordance (a daily highlighted entry) so first-timers see a door
opened before facing the alphabet. And restraint: no AI summaries of who Herman was.
Her pages answer; we only fetch them.

### Concept D — THE VANISHED WING

**Core idea.** A single dark room within the site dedicated to ON THE ROAD and STREET
SCENES: each facade painting hung beside its real, geocoded place — and, where the
place is gone, beside her own sentence saying so. An exhibition about disappearance,
which is what she said those paintings were about.

**Why the book demands it.** Her ON THE ROAD opener (p. 63) is explicit: she
"collected these fast-disappearing places," charmed by "vanishing qualities in our era
of franchise and 'up-scaling.'" The captions are already elegies: "THE MEADOWS…
The Last Roadhouse in our area, maybe the last anywhere. It has vanished." (p. 67).
Even the memoir mourns places: "Caleta beach is now a sewer" (p. 19). MEEKERS WELDING
(p. 65), MRS. WONG ON CLEMENT ST. (p. 111), DEMARTILE BROS., FISH BAIT SHACK, SAV-A-LOT
(LIST OF PAINTINGS, p. 289) — a census of a California that franchise erased. The
paintings are the only remaining record of most of these buildings; the site should
say that out loud.

**How a visitor moves.** Threshold: her p. 63 manifesto alone on a dark screen. Inside,
one long road: paintings hung in a row like storefronts on a strip, each with its
geocoded pin on a spare, desaturated Northern-California map running quietly beneath
the hang like a baseboard. Stop at a painting and the room gives you: her caption,
the place name, and one austere line of fact — "Painted in Pittsburg, California."
Where her own text records the loss, her sentence is the label's last line, set apart.
No street-view comparisons, no then/now sliders — the absence is stated, not
illustrated, because the painting must remain the only image of the place.

**Hero moment.** Standing in front of THE MEADOWS, reading "It has vanished" in her
words, and seeing the pin on the map of a road the visitor has actually driven.
People leave and tell someone: "She painted all the roadside places before they were
torn down, and the site shows you exactly where each one stood."

**Materials.** The 68 geocoded places; ON THE ROAD + STREET SCENES paintings (~40
works); her captions and the two chapter openers; dominant-color palettes to tint the
room per painting (her colors, nothing generated).

**What's hard.** Restraint against gimmickry — the moment this becomes a Google-Maps
mashup it's dead; the map must behave like architecture, not interface. Geocoding
precision varies ("near Pittsburgh, California"); labels must be honest about
approximation. And the temptation to add present-day photos must be refused on
principle: hers is the record.

### Concept E — THE TWENTY-MINUTE HANG
*(the docent tour)*

**Core idea.** A timed, auto-paced exhibition visit — twenty minutes, one sitting —
where the site walks you through a curated route of ~30 works and the only voice is
Leah's own captions and prose, sequenced like a docent tour with real rests. The
anti-feed: you give it your attention, it gives you a complete experience with an exit.

**Why the book demands it.** The book is a bounded object with a designed duration —
it opens, crescendos, rests, and *ends* (the index, then quiet endpapers). Its captions
are literally docent speech: first-person, anecdotal, timed to one painting at a time
(p. 227 Honfleur "sipping a pastis at a sidewalk café"; p. 255 the Bodrum shock-washer
joke). And her Introduction promise — "no ARTSPEAK" (p. 1) — is a tour-guide's ethic.
Infinite-scroll archives have no ending; her book does, and endings are what make
exhibitions memorable.

**How a visitor moves.** They press one button: "Visit." The site dims, a route begins —
threshold, painting, her caption fading in as if spoken, dwell time proportional to the
work's density (the tour lingers on SELF PORTRAIT, moves lightly through travel
sketches), an empty white screen every five or six works as a bench. A small clock-less
progress arc is the only chrome. At minute twenty: the dedication page, alone, as the
exit through the gift shop of feeling. Multiple routes shipped over time ("First Visit,"
"The Vanishing Route," "Herman's Route" — each assembled only from her sequences and
captions). Pause/resume; tap to linger; prefers-reduced-motion gets a self-paced
version with suggested dwell marks.

**Hero moment.** The ending. A site that *concludes* — dedication page, fourteen words,
done — is so rare that visitors describe the experience the way they describe an actual
exhibition: "I did the twenty-minute visit and it ended on the dedication to her
husband and I sat there for a second."

**Materials.** Curated subsets of plates; captions and prose verbatim; the photographs
for one biographical route; chapter structure for route-building.

**What's hard.** Auto-pacing is one bad dwell-time away from feeling like a slideshow
from 2009 — the timings need the obsessive tuning of a film edit, and a tap must always
hand control back instantly. Accessibility demands a fully self-paced equivalent. And
editorial nerve: a twenty-minute product that refuses to show you everything is a hard
sell to anyone who measures engagement in clicks — it must be sold as what it is, the
difference between a museum visit and a storage rack.

## How these fit together

If forced to build one thing: **B is the building, A is its physics, C is its second
entrance.** Twelve Rooms gives the site its architecture and Leah's own sequence;
Actual Size is a law applied inside every room rather than a separate destination; the
Index wall is the door for the returning visitor and the wanderer. The Vanished Wing is
the one room within B worth over-investing in, and the Twenty-Minute Hang is the
opening-night program that teaches people how to visit. The book already made all the
curatorial decisions in 1990. Our job is to build the rooms she could only afford in
paper.
