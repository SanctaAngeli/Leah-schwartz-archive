## IMMIGRANTS
*genetic baggage*

I am a product of the turn-of-the-century wave of middle-European
immigrants who brought Bagels and Lox to the "Aboriginal" Yankees.
They were people of talent, intelligence and industriousness who were
fleeing the terrible pogroms of that period. They came to America in the
crowded holds of ships filled with hope and dreams of Freedom.
My mother's family, the Pannemanskis (renamed Greenfield at Ellis
Island), emigrated from Poland, first to England, and then to America. In
London the four youngest children went to school. From this brief but
excellent education they learned proper English, good basic arithmetic,
exquisite handwriting, a firm grasp of grammatic construction and a life-
long love of reading. My mother told me she also worked in a cigarette
factory when she was seven, enjoyed the work and felt proud of doing it
well. My grandfather and his oldest son, in the usual immigrant pattern,
went to the U.S. first to earn enough money to bring the rest of the family
to America-The-Golden-Land. Why they settled in Rock Island, Illinois,
I don't know.

<!-- page 17 -->

My mother, uncles, and aunts were all talented people who painted
or made things with skill and artistry. My grandfather and my oldest uncle
were master cabinetmakers. My youngest uncle was a talented artist who
made a considerable fortune in business, but earlier in his life had a
portrait studio with ceiling and walls covered with black velvet, and twin-
kling "stars" in the ceiling. Such glamour! The family members were all
idealistic socialists, but not above capitalistic ventures of varying degrees
of profitability.
I was born in Rock Island, Illinois, on July 28, 1920 at a quarter to
twelve. My mother, as all mothers do, said I was a beautiful baby.
My real father, William Ammerman, disappeared from my life quickly
as my mother divorced him when I was an infant. This was the first
divorce in the family and considered so shameful that his name was never
mentioned, he was cut out of group pictures, and it wasn't until after my
mother died that I found this picture,
after having seen this one all my life.
He was a visionary socialist who was so sure that THE REVOLUTION
was 'just around the corner' that he handed out political tracts to his
potential customers instead of selling them insurance.

## CHICAGO
*divorced, alone, with an infant to support,*

Mother returns to Chicago
My mother moved to Chicago after her divorce to return to her old job
as self-taught bookkeeper at Montgomery Ward. She tried putting me in a
child-care home, and it was there that I had my earliest experience of
misery and humiliation. I recall being laughed at and called nosey because
I fell down the cellar stairs and got a nose bleed. I was given half an
orange to shut me up, and I still remember the taste of blood, tears and
orange. The doll my mother had given me for comfort was taken by a big
bully (probably six years old) and broken. Years later when I told my
mother about it she said, "You can't possibly remember that, you were
only two!" "But," I said, "I was there so long." She was shocked, and said,
"You were only there two days." Two days is a big chunk of a two-year-
old's life.
My mother was sometimes thoughtless, but never cruel; she took me
out of that frightening place and used her considerable ingenuity to invent
some work she could do at home. Her first brilliant idea was a BEADING
COLLEGE. In the early twenties there was a shortage of beaders to satisfy
the craze for beaded clothes, purses, shoes, belts, lampshades, and even
gloves. So, first she taught herself how to sew beads to fabrics, then she
had ornate diploma certificates printed, advertised that the College of
Beading was now accepting students, and guaranteed that even unskilled
young women would soon graduate as accomplished beaders. Her biggest
problem was creating a 'curriculum' for such a simple process.
After saturating Chicago with beaders, she thought up another survival
gambit. Mother's sister, Fanny, had married Sam Steiner, a wholesale
furniture dealer, so mother decided to become one of his furniture outlets.
She filled her five-room apartment with furniture on consignment from
Sam Steiner's warehouse. She sewed curtains, bedspreads, and, of course,
beaded lampshades. One bedroom was a fantasy of cerise satin, gathered
and puffed, with bands and edgings of gold brocade, with a matching
over-the-headboard-lamp with beaded fringe. On the walls she hung large,
ornately framed reproductions of popular paintings: Vigee Lebrun's
"Mother and Daughter," Rosa Bonheur's "The Horse Fair," and a
marvelous painting, "Aurora," of a bare-breasted goddess driving a golden
chariot drawn by great leaping horses across a glorious sky.

religious and, even more shocking, she read books.

man who wore a silver grey fedora and always had small pieces of candy in
the pocket of his velvet-collared overcoat. At five-and-a-half I took ballet

three-quarters I got a baby sister, Betty. Between the ages of six and ten, I
survived: diphtheria, heart murmur, tonsillectomy, measles, mumps,
whooping cough, scarlet fever, moving to Los Angeles, and seven differ-
ent schools. In spite of erratic attendance, I managed to learn how to read
and write, how to spell Smyrna, and sing "Frere Jacques". To my mother's
lasting chagrin, I never learned my times tables.

## LOS ANGELES, CALIFORNIA
*always the new kid at school*

In Los Angeles we moved a lot. Those were Depression years and we
moved to wherever my father could find work. We had few possessions.
We lived in furnished houses or apartments which my mother transformed
into "homes" with the help of familiar fabrics and pillows carried from
place to place in an old trunk. The trunk itself became the base of a "cozy
corner," padded with blankets, covered with a shawl, and stacked with
pillows. It was a place where I could read or do homework, and was
sometimes a bed for my baby sister. That trunk (smelling of mothballs,
and containing, along with the other stuff, an old fox fur complete with
toenails and glass eyes) was a reassuring object; to Betty and me, it was
the one familiar thing in a series of strange and varied environments.
Mother was handy at everything. She taught me how to sew, to make
electrical repairs, and to be intolerant of shoddiness. She admired good
workmanship and never passively accepted things that were broken, ugly,
or inconvenient. If she didn't know how to fix something, she learned
how. She was frugal without being mean-spirited. Daddy was hopeless at
repairs and not at all frugal, but was a better cook than mother when he
had the time.
Our repeated moves caused me intense suffering. I was an extremely
shy child, and death frequently seemed preferable to another strange
school. I don't think my mother ever understood how dreadful these
moves were for me because she craved new places and new people. One
time, to avoid going to a new school, I tried to catch "nemonia" by
running out in the cold with wet hair - according to folk wisdom, a sure-
fire way out of this vale of tears. No luck. However, at the age of ten I
discovered Public Libraries and found an endless supply of vicarious living.
(Bless you, Andrew Carnegie. I'm sure you are at the right hand of God,
in charge of the celestial archives.) I read all the "Twin" books — German
Twins, French, Dutch, Scotch, Japanese and Swiss. Books on barnstorm-
ing, horses, Albert Payson Terhune's dog books, even a book on Tinto-
retto's childhood that described his use of crushed flowers when he
couldn't afford paint (I tried it, it didn't work). I read books with words
I had never heard spoken, so I had a vocabulary that I didn't know how to
pronounce. Anemone I pronounced in my head as annie-moan and misled
as meizzld.
The year the Lindbergh baby was kidnapped (I remember newsboys
yelling "EXTRA! EXTRA!") we lived in Venice, California in a little
clapboard house a block from the beach. The house was on the Speedway,
a narrow busy street with no sidewalks, where traffic rushed by a few
inches from our front porch. The house was furnished with dirty-white
wicker furniture, lumpy with sandy, faded, cretonne cushions, I slept in
a cretonne-curtained alcove bed.
I was mad for the beach. I would rush out early in the morning, hoping
for low tide with its vast shining stretch of wet sand dotted with bits of
shells and seaweed. When there was a "red tide" we would gather purple
jellyfish and stomp them into holes in the sand and then use the purple
sand for very fancy creations. My best friend was a Japanese girl whose
family owned a restaurant on the boardwalk. She was beautiful and could
do handsprings and backbends.
At one time we lived near the Temple of the Cult Goddess, Aimee
Semple McPherson. Many of our neighbors wore the cult uniform which
included a flowing navy blue cape, which I thought rather romantic, until
the loutish son-of-a-blue-cape called me a "dirty jew." I couldn't under-
stand why. I was clean, and every time we moved my mother was meticu-
lous in leaving the premises cleaner than we found them. When I asked
her why she was scrubbing the floor before we moved, she said, "So
nobody can call us 'dirty jews.'"
When my father got a job working on a big chicken ranch, we moved
to Reseda in the San Fernando valley. The valley was filled with apricot
and walnut orchards and surrounded by clearly visible beautiful moun-
tains. For a pittance, we rented a house with an acre of land, a chicken
run, and a huge vegetable garden and grape arbor. Every Sunday my
father would kill a rooster, my mother would take out the iridescent guts,
scald the legs and peel them, and boil the hell out of the tough old bird.
If she was feeling particularly creative she would make a fricassee. My
mother, being quite a flirt, had a "boy friend" who always managed to
arrive on Sunday afternoon just as we were about to sit down to dinner.
He gave me the creeps. One Sunday afternoon, looking disgustingly fur-
tive, he grabbed me and kissed me on the lips. UGH! (I was 8 or 9.)
Aside from feeling terribly embarrassed for him, I was sure that I had
contracted one of those diseases that were never mentioned out loud.
This is how we got our first automobile. An old Chevy drove up our
gravel driveway one day and, to our astonishment, Daddy was alone in the
car and behind the wheel. Mother shrieked, "Nate, when did you learn to
drive?!" "I just did," he said proudly. "I got here, didn't I?" (Mother was a
little miffed that he bought the car without consulting her). The truth is,
he never did learn to drive very well. He held his arms straight out from

When I was ten, we lived for a while in a "court," a U-shaped string
of apartments around a long courtyard with palm trees down the center.
The tenants were an odd bunch. A girl my age who lived across the way
showed me her father's stash of dirty pictures (I was fascinated, shocked
and disgusted). The young red-headed woman next door had an awful lot
of "boyfriends" (day and night), and played "Good Night Sweetheart" on
the Victrola til dawn.
We soon moved again, to a more wholesome neighborhood near the
new Olympic Stadium where the 1932 games were to be held. Our
landlords lived in the front part of the house and we had the back, which
included a glassed-in sun porch that was my bedroom. My,parents slept in
the living room on a bed that disappeared into a large drawer during the
day. The landlord kept rabbits in the backyard under an old fig tree, and
every Sunday they had fried rabbit (it smelled lovely).
Albert Payson Terhune's books about noble, clever, heroic collies had
warped my mind. I felt that my life could never be fulfilled unless I had a
dog (preferably a collie). My mother felt otherwise. One day I saw an
adorable fox terrier puppy in a pet store window and bought it with my
life savings, two dollars. I hoped that Mother would be so enchanted by
the dear little thing that she would let me keep him. The puppy promptly
threw up and had diarrhea all over the floor. Mother was not enchanted.
In tears, I took the puppy back to the pet store, but the pet store man
wouldn't even give my money back.
My parents must have been able to save up some money because my
father and a partner opened the first "supermarket" in L.A. Daddy ran the
meat and fish department and his partner was in charge of the grocery and
produce department. Mother helped out in the store so I was on my own
a lot.
On March 10, 1933 I went to fetch my sister from a neighbor's house
a few blocks away. We were in their backyard when THE BIG QUAKE
struck. The ground heaved and shook, a powerful rumbling roar came out
of the ground, but I wasn't frightened — just excited. Later, during the
many aftershocks, I saw fear on Daddy's face and from then on I was
terrified of earthquakes; even the vibrations of a passing truck could, make
me shudder with fear.
When I was twelve, I was allowed to go to the Saturday matinee at the
neighborhood movie house. After I got dressed up in my red voile wrap-
around blouse, white skirt and white "Olympic Sandals," I was given fif-
teen cents — ten for the movie and five for a double decker ice cream
cone. Oh, what enchantment! I would come out of the theater in a trance.
Iwas Greta Garbo (or Dietrich, Lombard, or Harlow). I was loved by
Clark Gable (or Ronald Colman, Fredric March or Gene Raymond).
I floated home and bored my mother into a stupor with an interminable
description of every detail of plot, music, costumes, and verbatim
dialogue. I must have driven her crazy, but she never told me to shut up.
The movies were TOTAL GLAMOUR. The theatres were clean, sweet-
smelling and elegant. Most homes were not as beautiful as the movie
houses, so going to the movies was more than just seeing the film, it was
an environment of luxury: plush seats, thick carpets, romantic murals, gold
and crystal chandeliers, and marble restrooms. Movie Palaces really were
palaces then. Now, with feet stuck to the floor, on a seat stiff with spilled
Pepsi, the air reeking with rancid popcorn oil, one can watch heads ten
feet high eating each others mouths (Romance), nude bodies huffing and
heaving (Love), disembowelments, decapitations, slime-balls from outer
space-(Action-Adventure), car chases, crashes, pratfalls, and squashed
animals (Comedy). In spite of all that, I think a lot of movies are better
now than they were then.

## MICHIGAN
*how I stopped learning French and started shovelling manure*

In Los Angeles, I was in junior high, studying French, wearing nail
polish and noticing "cute" boys when my mother announced that we were
moving to a collective farm in Michigan. I couldn't stand it; I couldn't
believe it; I couldn't IMAGINE living on a farm with bugs and snakes
and dirt and manure. Contrary to my expectations I loved it, and it turned
out to be a significant influence on the rest of my life. I learned to ignore
bugs (some more than others), I collected garter snakes (to scare people
with), and I shoveled manure (anything to be near horses).
MOTHER AND DADDY ON THE WAY
TO MICHIGAN
We moved to Michigan because my mother believed in the perfecti-
bility of mankind. "Sunrise Farm" was a collective near Saginaw on ten
thousand acres of rich bottom-land crisscrossed with drainage canals
and levees which were topped with good gravel roads connecting three
hamlets.
"Alicia" was the main hamlet, the site of adult housing, communal
dining room, children's house, creamery and dairy barns, horse barn, silos
and warehouses.
The second hamlet was the,center for the harvesting and distilling of
Peppermint. When the wind was right, the distillery could be smelled a
mile away.
The third hamlet was the sheep ranch, with vast barns for shearing and
lambing, and houses for the shepherds. The farm had been stocked by its
previous owners with purebred animals of the highest quality, including a
great many enormous and beautiful prize-winning Belgian Draft Horses.
They became my passion. The new "farmers" were lefties of every shade
from pale pink to deepest red (with a few anarchists thrown in for good
measure) who were filled with passionate certainties. They were True
Believers, skilled in polemics, late-night argumentations, disputations,
protests, dialectics, history of the Labor Movement, what Lenin ate for
breakfast, and every detail of the Russian Revolution. What they didn't
know was how to milk a cow, feed a horse, or plant a carrot. The socialist
motto, "To each according to his need, from each according to his
ability," was taken to heart by Mr. Tannenbaum, whose need was to be
taken care of and whose ability consisted of strutting about in fancy city
clothes (spats, cane and splendid grey fedora) and distracting hard-
working women with ornate compliments.
I shared a small slant-ceilinged room with a girl my age. The toilet,
bath and potbellied stove were downstairs, and a glass of water would
freeze in our room in the midst of winter. My roommate and I were not
particularly compatible, but I was out in the barns all day while she
practiced the violin, so we managed. Her wild-haired mother lived in
the same house and always had jars of souring milk on her windowsills.
In the evenings a group of us teenagers would get together with a
young graduate student who taught us basic science, atomic theory, read
us "Microbe Hunters,' and generally opened our minds to the wonders of
our world. He was a remarkably good teacher and I've been grateful to
him all my life. I didn't go to school, but learned infinitely more than if
I had.
This is how I learned to ride. I told Jim, one of the horse handlers, that
I wanted to learn how to ride. Jim said,"OK, the saddle's there, and the
horse is in his stall," and he walked away. After I messed around with the

bridle for a while, trying to get the bit between the horse's teeth, Jim
relented and showed me how to do it, and walked away again. I was
terrified of the horse's huge villainous teeth, but would have perished
rather than admit it. I got the saddle blanket and saddle on OK and was
about to try and mount when Jim reappeared and lightly tugged at the
saddle; it slid around and hung under the horse's belly. I was impressed.
He then showed me how to wait for the horse to exhale and to quickly
tighten the cinch, and he walked away.
Now I was ready to swing myself gracefully into the saddle and Hi Ho
Silver and away, but somehow the horse had become twelve feet tall and
I had to use an overturned bucket as a step-up. Once in the saddle I didn't
know how to make the horse move. I said, "Giddyup," but nothing
happened. I flapped the reins, nothing happened. In my frustration, I
must have touched his flank with my heel because he ambled out of the
barn and stopped. Well, by trial and error and the help of some free
Government booklets, I learned to ride and even found that something
called "posting" made it possible to ride at a trot with minimal agony.
SHOWING OFF ON "MAX"
I learned to milk cows, and milked five cows at six a.m. and six p.m. In
winter it was pitch dark and bitter cold at those times, but the barn was
warm and fragrant, and the cows had a velvet-soft "hand-warmer"
between their udder and hind leg.
I worked in the fields (when I couldn't get out of it), hand-weeding
peppermint with a large weeding crew. We "swept" the field of weeds
before the crop was cut, dried, and taken to the distillery for the extraction
of the valuable oil which, with sugar beets, was our main cash crop.
In lambing season I hung out in the sheep barns, fascinated by the mass
dropping of lambs, and was sometimes allowed to help in a minor way,
like tying a dead lamb's bloody skin onto an orphan lamb so the dead
lamb's mother would accept it.
One day, while pitching hay down to the horses with an "older man"
(age 18), we stopped for a moment and he held me against the wall and
kissed me. WOW! I mooned around for days trying to be near him
without his knowing it and terrified of coming face to face with him
because I didn't know how to act. I sensed that it meant nothing to him,
but for me it was cataclysmic... that kiss released a cataract of hormones
that had been lying in wait for some such catalyst. The farm was steamy
with sexual activity, great fat Belgian stallions servicing docile mares,
rutting sheep, cows in heat, and suddenly I knew what it all meant.
I drew a lot of horses during the farm years, some from life and some
copied from farm journals. I also painted some landscapes on cardboard
with cheap poster paint. I was in "competition" with a boy my age who
painted landscapes that were praised for their "interesting" color... he was
color blind. I hated him.
We left Sunrise Farm abruptly. One day my mother told me, without
explanation, to get my things together as we were leaving the following
day. I was devastated. I cried myself to sleep for months afterward. After
all the moving around and changing schools and homes and neighbor-
hoods, I believed that we were in our permanent home so I had sprouted
roots and made friends. Most maddening and grievous was leaving "my"
horse. I was grooming a strawberry roan Belgian filly for the State Fair.
I had watched her being born, had selected her from a dozen others, had
broken her to halter, and loved her madly.
A month later, a friend wrote me that my horse had won the blue
ribbon in her class at the State Fair.
There was no reprieve. My mother, father, Betty and I piled into our
old car with bundles tied on the roof and "The Trunk" tied to the back,
and we took off for a new "Utopia" in New Jersey.

BETTY AND THE OLD CHEVY IN MICHIGAN
We Homestead kids went to Hightstown High School for the first year
and then, true to my Karma, were transferred to Allentown High for the
second year. Allentown was in the heart of rich, red-neck, potato farming
territory. Some of the farm boys even had their own cars, a rare distinction
in those Depression years.
A few months into the new term, a farm kid on the way to his next
class snarled, "JEW!" at one of our brightest. Our Brightest, without a
nanosecond hesitation, withered him with a scathingly contemptuous,
"GENTILE!" The hostility between these two different cultures was not
improved by the "Jew Kids" walking away with all the scholastic honors.
After a big hassle (no girl hadever even wanted to), I was allowed to
take the agriculture and shop classes, where I got all A's (How to be
unpopular in high school!). I didn't care about the local louts; I was madly
in love with the teacher, Mr. Stevenson, who looked like Henry Fonda.
JERSEY HOMESTEADS
(Roosevelt N.J.)
another farm and two more schools
The new utopia, in the potato farming area of New Jersey, was created
by the Resettlement Administration of Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal
program. The government had built a community of modern, well
designed houses, a big modern factory, and provided hundreds of acres of
fertile farm land for the resettlement of garment workers from New York
City. The community was supposed to operate as a three-way cooperative
of farm, store, and factory in order to decentralize the garment industry
from New York. HAH! Aside from the cockeyed premise, the people
themselves were hardly the stuff of entrepreneurism. They were voluble,
well-read, of a leftish persuasion (but not as far out in left field as the
Sunrise Farmnicks), and loved, with a passion, recreational disputation.
They could stay up all night arguing fine points of mischievous nonsense.
Needless to say, few if any of them knew anything about farming or even
running a factory. Daddy ran the food store.
ART SCHOOL, LOVE

## AND MARRIAGE
*confusion, angst, subjugation*

After graduating from high school, I was free to realize my lifelong
dream of going to art school. There was one tuition-free school in New
York City: The National Academy on 110th St. near the Cathedral of St.
John the Divine. My family could spare only five dollars a week for my
expenses, so I had to stay with my mother's cousins in the Bronx. They
lived in a large, elegant apartment building in a "good" neighborhood, and
led an extremely well-ordered, conventional life. I must have seemed like a
primitive throwback — used to farm life, rough language and unsuper-
vised freedom. These relatives were kind, loving people who felt that a
young girl should have close supervision; they even insisted on a carefully
kept telephone log, number called, time, and duration of call. In summer,
white slipcovers were put on sofa and chairs, and rugs were put in storage
— all very strange and intimidating to me.
I felt so restricted that one day I rented a tacky furnished room in a
despicable neighborhood for two dollars a week — leaving three dollars
for food, transportation, and art supplies.

The subway cost five cents, you could get a huge sandwich with
coleslaw and a pint of milk for twenty cents, and at school we were using
cheap paper and charcoal during the first term. I thought I could get by.
Cousin Frank was scandalized! He called my mother and persuaded her
to make me come home.
Hallelujah! I was saved in the nick of time by an ad on the bulletin
board at school. "Wanted, young woman to help with babies in exchange
for room and board. Call So and So at such and such a number." And so
I met two sisters who lived with their husbands and babies in a huge,
upper-Manhattan apartment.
This was my first exposure to young New York "sophisticates" who
went to foreign films, listened to classical music, cooked delicious food,
and had modern Danish furniture and ethnic artifacts (very avant garde in
1938). I learned a great deal from them.
MY FIRST ART SCHOOL PAINTING
(just like the teacher's)
The Academy was tiresome with it's curriculum of nothing but plaster
cast drawing for the first year, so through Harry Shapiro (one of "the
husbands" who was a commercial artist) I got a scholarship to the Ameri-
can Artists School on Fourteenth Street. It was a hotbed of lefties, so I felt
right at home. We plunged directly into oil painting, no fooling around
with learning how to draw, perspective, color, painting techniques... just
paint like the teacher. So I did. I turned out a bunch of "Sol Wilson's" in
a vaguely impressionistic, amorphous style, and learned to wash my
brushes properly. I have always had difficulty learning from formal "teach-
ers." I seem to retain things much better when I figure them out for my-
self, but this also means that I am always disconnected from the "main-
stream." While going to art school I had two boyfriends, both from New
Jersey. I was in love with Lee Drasin, who was going to Cornell in upstate
New York. I was extremely fond of Gene Brown, who had a well-paying
job in New York and took me out to lovely places to eat. I discovered
prawns, lobster, French cheeses, gin, sukiyaki, curry, pate, and real Italian
spaghetti. I had never tasted such things; my mother's version of spaghetti
was fried onions and Campbell's Tomato soup over Maneshevitz noodles!
The only cheeses I knew were Velveeta, cream cheese, cottage cheese, and
sliced American.
When Lee came down to New York City from Cornell, we were
sometimes given theater tickets by his sister Tamara, who was starring in a
hit Broadway musical. She was the one who sang "Smoke Gets in Your
Eyes" in the musical "Roberta." Tamara was married to a wealthy advertis-
ing man who advised me to change my name to Dubina (from Dubin) if
I wanted to become a famous artist. I scorned such tactics, with youthful
"purity," but he was probably right.

I was impressed and a bit intimidated by Tamara with her exquisite
clothes, perfumes, and glamorous terraced Penthouse. This bright and airy
apartment was furnished with elegant furniture covered in rich fabrics;
original Boudin watercolors and Degas drawings hung on the walls. The
dining room had a tiled floor and was a sumptuous version of a French
farm kitchen. One day I had lunch there with Tamara and a little, skinny

## TIME TO PAINT
*if everything else is taken care of*

I'm amazed that I managed to paint at all during those years, but I did.
I had my first one-man show in San Francisco during the sixties, and
many more over the years. The big problem for me was "openings." I used
to get smashed to overcome my shyness. My "Ideal" is to paint to my
heart's content and drop the finished work down a hole in the floor and let
someone else deal with it. But that's not the way the world works.
When Peter and Davy were in high school (in my forties), I painted in-
tensely for months at a time and would then hit a "slump," which would
usually pass in a month or two. Twice I've had year-long "painter's block."
During one of these painful times, I took ceramics classes at the local
college and learned to throw pots on the wheel, a satisfying accomplish-
ment. During another "slump-time," I studied photography, turned a
bathroom into a darkroom, and spent days trying to make good prints
from lousy negatives because I could never master the "zone system"
which deals with numbers for calculating perfect exposures (I think I'm a
numbers dyslexic). Being "number-blind" has been an embarrassment all
my life. Phone numbers, addresses, prices and dates are like a foreign
language I can memorize, but with only partial success. My husband,
luckily, is a numbers genius. He and our son Peter (who also has a head
for numbers) claim that they perceive numbers as different colors. I've
never heard of that from any other source. These dilettantish diversions
were useful time fillers while the mysterious process of "creativity" was
lying low.

sometimes on the highway, sometimes through little farm towns and miles
of grey, picked-over cotton fields. On one of these trips I photographed

Daddy recovered from his heart attack, but in the hospital they
discovered he had bowel cancer. We brought him up to San Francisco
Kaiser Hospital, where Herman could supervise his care and make sure
that he was not denied necessary pain-killers. He died in the hospital here
and was buried in Los Angeles next to old friends from New Jersey.
Mother stayed with us for awhile, but we drove each other crazy. She
could not stand to be alone for a moment and was totally gregarious,
while I have always needed long periods of solitude in order to work and
maintain sanity.
I took her back to her home at Lake Elsinore but she felt miserable
there, she tried San Diego and was miserable there, I took her to look at
retirement hotels in Santa Monica but that wasn't her kind of place, so she
finally went back to Lake Elsinore. Mother had a tragic flaw. She made
friends easily, almost "falling in love," lavishing presents and attentions on
the new friend, but time and again was disappointed when the new friend
backed off a bit. Mother's "infatuation" then turned into fault-finding and
disappointment. Elsinore had too many of these failed relationships. As I
write this, I may have discovered the real reason Mother had to move so
frequently. She loved being "infatuated" but didn't know how to sustain
a friendship, so she ran away from it.
Two years later, when we were in London, I got a call from Betty say-
ing that Mother had been operated on and was being taken care of by
Daddy's nephew Dr. Sam Steindel (internist) in a private hospital in Los
Angeles. When we got home I flew down to see her and relieve Betty so
she could return to her husband and the care of her three children. I found
that Sam's colleague, a surgeon, was planning another operation as soon
as Mother recovered enough. I was shocked when I saw her; she looked
other-worldly, her face unlined, her high cheekbones more prominent,
and her teeth too large, looking like the ancient Egyptian Queen Nefertiti.
She was extremely weak, glad to see me, and said firmly that she did not
want another operation.
I didn't know what to do about anything. I visited her for the few
minutes allowed, three times a day, and the rest of the time drifted around
L.A. taking buses, feeling sad for mother, for me, and for all the lonely old
people sitting on mortuary benches. At a bus stop under a hot, grey sun,
a scrawny old black woman told me that "It's so hot 'cause THEY lowered
the mountain, and now the sun come down too close."

down to L.A. and "kidnap" her. She was pathetically grateful and a bit of

We brought her to the Kaiser "cottage" hospital in San Rafael where
she was kept comfortable and where she healed from her surgery. I had
hoped to be able to bring her home, but she was too weak to move. I
knew she was dying. When a woman in her eighties gets pancreatic cancer
there is no cure. Repeated operations are just busy work for the surgeon,
and futile agony for the patient.
A few days before she died she held my hand and said, "I'm so lucky."
She never complained, said the nurses were angels and a sip of water was
heavenly.
Her whole life had been a search for the IDEAL. She never found her
ideals of noble friendship and unselfish community but always believed in
the possibility of such perfection. No reality of disappointment ever made
her cynical. She looked into each new face with an assurance that this new
person was kind and good, and would respond to her smile with an
answering smile of friendship.
Throughout her life, from working in a factory as a child through the
Depression years to the bitter loss of her husband, she maintained an
attitude of optimism and bravery that was an example to her children and
grandchildren. She admired people with a zest for life. She was one of
those gifted people. She was buried in L.A. next to Daddy and their dear
old friends. Peter and Davy were wonderfully helpful with the sad job of
sorting, saving and getting rid of mother's household things, and Betty
saved me from the social obligation of attending mother's funeral.

## WORK
*the accidental watercolorist*

One fine day, while browsing the shelves of the Marin County Library,
I found a large volume of color photographs of beetles from all over the
world. I was so taken with their color and bizarre forms that I went tight
out and bought watercolors, brushes and paper and tried to paint them.
I hadn't painted with watercolors since I was a kid, but I remembered
reading that one should wet the whole paper first, so I did, and painted
whole reams of soft-edged messes before I learned a little control. The
trick with watercolor is to let the color flow but not to let it get away from
you. The battle between letting go and control is the basic way of water-
color.
But, of course, watercolor is never conquered. There are traps and
pitfalls waiting at every brushstroke, and failures often outnumber
successes. I once ruined the simple background I was aiming for in a
flower painting, exasperating because the flowers had turned out fine. I
looked up at a Bellini reproduction that I had pinned to the studio wall
and decided to paint it "behind" the flowers. It was fun "getting into
Bellini's mind," so I painted several other flower paintings with
backgrounds of other famous paintings (see Flower section).
For many years I worked exclusively in watercolor, and enjoyed
teaching my self-taught methods to others. I've tried to make my students
(many of them exhibiting artists in other media) aware of the ordinary
things around them. I encourage students to "listen" to themselves and to
try to paint from direct, original and focused observation, and to resist the
tendency to paint a painting that looks like a million others — i.e., the
picturesque boat, barn or winebottle and drape... the curse of watercolor

PETER, HERMAN, ME AND DAVY (1972)
As a woman painter who married, raised children, and kept house (with
all that that entails), I have found the most precious work-necessity to be
continuity, and like most precious things, it is rare.
Discontinuity causes great problems for a painter, or any artist. If a
pianist misses practice or if a ballet dancer doesn't work out regularly, it is
disastrous. You can't shut the factory down and expect to jump right back
into production when you have a few moments to spare. Interruptions
and distractions are deadly.
I remember one evening when the sky was filled with the most en-
chanting wispy clouds (a rarity here), I rushed down to the back yard with
my watercolor gear, took a deep breath, and was about to lay down a
meltingly gorgeous blue wash when my two adorable sons started their
usual squabbling, two feet from where I sat. I felt an enormous kinship
with mothers who bludgeon their children to death.
Imogen Cunningham, the photographer, once said she had never done
landscapes because in the early morning or evening when the light was
best, she was feeding her family.
When I was trying to write notes for this book, the Dutch Elm Disease
Warden dropped by, a phone call announced the unexpected arrival of a
friend from Africa, the house painters were just leaving, I was distracted
by thoughts of preparation for the arrival of my ex-daughter-in-law with
two small children, and the painting I had started the week before never
did get finished.
So, here's a portion of my life's work that did get finished.
