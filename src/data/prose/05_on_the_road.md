## ON THE ROAD
*facades of obscure lives*

When my kids started going to school full-time, I spent a lot of time
roaming around the countryside, painting landscapes from my Ford
Econoline van.
One day near Pittsburgh, California, I saw "Meekers Welding" and was
so taken by it's intense red paint job that it became the first in the series of
"On The Road" paintings. I "collected" these fast-disappearing places
from all over the state. I generally work from black and white photographs
taken on location.
I love the significant detai s of these places: the Christmas decorations
on "CHRIS'S #2 CAFE," the large electric sign in front of "GEORGE
AND MARSHA'S PLACE," (that they must have brought with them
from Price, Utah), the profusion of hand lettered signs on "SAV-A-LOT"
and "FIREBAUGH BAIT SHOP," traces of old filling station in front of
"RAINBOW INN," the painted "stones" on the facades of "SAN
PEDRO CAFE" and "BUZZ'S."
I have always loved detail. When I first came to New York, I saw the
work of the Flemish painters in the Metropolitan Museum. My favorite
was a small, very busy painting of the crucifixion that had a magnifying
lens dangling on a chain next to it so that one could examine the fine
detail, which included a hammer, nails, and pliers at the foot of the cross.
These simple tools sent a chill up my spine, they made the crucifixion seem
so very real.
My interest in these roadside places is hard to define. I am not
interested in quaintness nor flamboyance, but am charmed by the idiosyn-
cracy, innocent simplicity of purpose, and individuality of these places -
vanishing qualities in our era of franchise and "up-scaling." I create
scenarios, in my mind, of the lives of the people involved with these
places. Every detail is part of the fabric of someone's life, so I spend a lot
of time getting the details right. A crumpled bit of trash has to look
authentic; it adds to the feeling of time and place. Some painters eliminate
detail to create a stronger emotional impact, but I like to elaborate and in-
tensify detail to achieve a heightened reality. These paintings are all water-
colors.
