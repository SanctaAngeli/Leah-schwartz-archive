## PORTRAITS
*beauty and self-possession*

I have drawn professional models who, though not beautiful, have such
vitality and self-possession that it is virtually impossible to do a bad
drawing of them, and then there are some beauties that are hopeless.
I once attempted a portrait of a beautiful, lively young friend. I realized
when she sat for me that I had never really "seen" her. She is so vivacious
that what one sees is movement, color and the constant display and
sharing of information. When she sits still, her 'light goes out'.
Maddening!
The first portrait I ever got paid for (I was nineteen or so) was of a
dazzling seven-year-old beauty with real auburn hair, creamy skin, and
golden eyes with dark lashes. Her mother paid me $25. My fortune was
made.
Except for the drawings at the end of this section, these paintings are
all watercolors.

(From a letter to Judy Adler, in Newfoundland, while I was working
on SELF PORTRAIT WITH BEVELLED MIRROR).
Dear Judy,
I keep thinking about you, in your snug cottage, surrounded by frozen
rocky shores ...It sounds very satisfying to me. I love the idea of spareness
and simplicity, yet I surround myself withglut and complications. I've
worked myself into a corner of my bedroom (after glutting-up my studio)
with my books and typewriter on the right and work table and watercolors
next to the window through which I can see the bay and San Francisco.
I've been working on a series of watercolors of a bunch of spring flowers
(with self-portrait in the background). At first the flowers are fresh and
bright and I am dim and dull ...but as the flowers fade I look better and
better ...Ha Ha, they fade faster than I do! I have a constant battle with
the miserable darkness of stormy weather. Artificial light distorts color so I
peer and squint and curse the rain, but I feel happy and vigorous because I
am painting.
