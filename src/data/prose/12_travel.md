TRAVEL
I used to be so petrified at the thought of flying that all I could
manage was to go on every other overseas trip with my travel-obsessed
husband. From the day I knew our departure date I was sick with
humiliating dread. When the day finally arrived (and I hadn't come down
with bubonic plague or broken a leg), I resigned myself to death and filled
up with valium, booze and sleeping pills. It's a wonder I survived my
remedies but I did, and have also overcome my fear of flying (87%)
if not my reluctance to leave home and treasured routine.
I travel light, with one carry-on bag and a small backpack with camera
and painting gear, small plastic paintbox, 2 good watercolor brushes,
sketch book, small water bottle, and a chocolate bar, all fitted into a quilted
tea-cozy which makes a good cushion. Oh yes, I also carry a pocket-size
dime store notebook for daily travel notes.
Since this is a book of many parts, I'm only showing a small fraction of
my hundreds of travel sketches, and a few excerpts from my travel note-
books.

FRANCE
We have, at various times, travelled through the Loire Valley, Brittany,
the Auvergne, Burgundy, Normandy, Bordeaux, the Riviera and a bit of
the French Alps, and loved it all. We seem to be on the move most of the
time, so there's not much time to paint unless I get up at dawn, which I
like to do anyway.
NOTES FROM LES EYZIE:
Walking through the woods with Herman, Maggie and Herb, we met
a tiny, shrivelled woman in a black dress, with one long tooth left in her
mouth, who told us that the path we were on led to a village where we
could get lunch. Two hours later we came upon a group of farm buildings,
and through an open door we could see farmers eating a bountiful,
aromatic noonday meal. Herb, missing his lunch, asked if they'd sell us
some bread - they refused. No village in sight, so we trudged down the
trail, mocked by two persistent cuckoos, through a forest of chestnut trees
which was carpeted with spring flowers, and came upon the ancient crone
again at the bottom of the hill. She showed us her home, which consisted
of one room carved into the limestone cliff (as many homes and hotels are
in this area). The room was almost filled with a large bed covered with a
great froth of eiderdown comforter. In front, on a limestone ledge, was a
mini-garden with healthy looking cosmos and broccoli. She was cheery
and bright as a sparrow, widowed for fifty-seven years.
HOTEL des GROTTES, CABRERET:
Up at first light, sketched the view of the village through our window -
very complicated, castle on the left, high limestone cliffs in the distance,
and the village sprawled across the foreground.
WEDNESDAY, MAY 28, LE PUY TO MURAT TO SALERS:
Rain, rain, rain. Saw herds of the handsome Salers cattle, sleek red with
sharp curving horns. Lunched at "Les Messageries" in Murat. Super!
Crudites with hard boiled egg and pickled mackerel, tender beef tongue
and boiled potatoes, and delectable strawberry tart for dessert.
Feeling well fortified, we continued on through rain-brightened,
elegant farm country to Salers on an escarpment in the heart of the
Auvergne. Stayed at the Hotel Bellfoi in a heated, low-ceilinged, cozy
room with ancient beams and a small deep-set window, through which we
could see wet, black, scalloped slate roofs and the distant misty green
valley. Eiderdown comforter on ample bed, a table and two chairs, our
own little pink and green tiled bathroom, what more could anyone want?
Next morning I felt travel-weary so I stayed under the eiderdown and read
til noon, while the heavy dark rain battered the slate roofs. Late in the
afternoon the sun came out and I circumnavigated the town feeling touchy
and "flayed" even though the sunlight made everything bright and joyous
looking. The next morning after bread, cheese and hot chocolate, I went
to the edge of the escarpment to paint the silver-green valley below but
the wind blew cold and the clouds returned.
PARIS, MAY 28:
From our hotel on the Ile de la Cite, crossed over the river to meet
Herman, Joyce, and the Rosens at Joe Plasket's in the Marais. Joe, a tall
gentle-faced artist (Maggie's friend), lives in an ancient, slightly crooked
and bulging corner house on the Rue Pacquay. A moldering limestone
doorway leads to a brick and wood spiral staircase which, on the first
complete turn, opens into the atelier-sitting room with a tall, narrow
window that diffuses the sunlight through a thick layer of dust - a
pleasant glowing effect. Every surface is covered with cloths, shawls,
books, magazines, old New Yorkers, The Joy Of Cooking, bibelots,
tchatchkes. The walls are hung with ornate crackled mirrors and Joe's
paintings. A ginger cat picks its way through a forest of flea market
treasures on top of a grand piano. Several layers of oriental rugs cover
the floor, vases of flowers in various stages of decay sit on piles of papers.
After sherry, we go up another turn of the stairway passing a little
bathroom seen through low cupboard doors, many paintings, a kitty litter
box and marble busts on pedestals, and enter Joe's bedroom. The bed,
huge pillows, walls and ceiling are covered with a king's ransom of
cashmere shawls - dark, dusty, and richly decadent. Again, every surface
is covered with little treasures - netsuke, vases, statues and magnificent
wooden angels form the foot of the bed. The next turn of the stairs brings
us to David's ante-room containing the Chinese Museum, which is a
small room jam-packed with cabinets filled with exquisite little Chinese
tchatchkes, fragments of embroidered silk, fans, ivories, and things too
varied and numerous to remember. Then David's Room! The bed and
bed alcove hung with folds upon folds of sumptuously colored velvety
material, the colors softened by the ubiquitous dust of years. A facing
panelled wall is painted by David, in trompe l'oeil in the grand old manner.
After this fascinating tour, we walked through a 'time warp' toward the
Beaubourg (Pompedou Center) with its high-tech blue and white conduits
high above the rooftops of the moldering houses on the Rue Pacquay.
The Beaubourg Exhibition Hall is an enormous mass of scaffolding,
wiring, tubing, bracing, ventilators, plastic-enclosed escalators, glass,
metal and plastic surfaces, harsh and exciting. It seems to generate waves
of energy as hordes of people enter and leave. From the top there is a
splendid panorama of Paris.

ITALY
September 7, 1974, Monday noon. Mill Valley-Oakland by car.
Oakland-Bangor-Gatwick by plane. Gatwick-Victoria Station-Dover by
train. Dover to Calais by boat in a force ten gale. Calais-Paris by train.
Arrive Paris 7p.m. Tuesday. Cab to Michelet Odeon Hotel (the Odious)
across the street from the Odeon Theatre.
Wednesday: Rent a very small, bright yellow Renault, stuff ourselves,
Herb and Maggie Rosen, and luggage into impossibly small car, and
tootle off over the Alps to Italy, down the Val d'Aosta to Bergamo. Herb
has tiny roasted birds on polenta for supper and we all shudder.
Leave Bergamo early, drive through milky vapors rising from vine-
covered fields, to Brescia. While Maggie and Herb look at art treasures,
Herman and I walk the narrow streets which are lined with sumptuous
food shops ...wild game, boar, pheasant, hare, venison, ...cheeses in
infinite variety ...take-out food, fried octopus, mussels in cream, clams,
squid, roasted song birds, poached salmon, shrimps en gelee, ...salads of
unimaginable variety, ...pastries, breads, rolls, tiny pizzas, and then to
the open-air markets selling everything from polenta to parakeets. Meet
Maggie and Herb back at the Grand Piazza, have espresso, squeeze back
into the Renault Lemon and head for Verona singing the opening song
from "Kiss Me Kate".
In Verona we stay at the Hotel Bologna. I paint a small watercolor of
the evening promenade while the others explore the town. M&H bring
Alfred Frankenstein (The Art Historian and art critic of the San Francisco
Chronicle) back to have dinner with us. He doesn't want to talk art with
Maggie and me but doctor stuff with Herman.
ARE ZZO:
(M&H in Rome for a few days.) Herman and I stayed at the Chiave
D'Oro on the Piazza San Francesca, next to a shabby, plain old church.
We dutifully looked inside, thinking it would be grand and glorious
(Schwartz's Law of Italian Churches), but it was quite bare, gloomy and
shabby, with a few barely-visible stained and crumbling frescos on the side
walls. Still curious, I walked all the way back to the chapel behind the altar
and in the dim light I saw the horses, banners, and lances of the Piero de la
Francesca frescos I had adored since I first saw a book of his work in my
teens. I was stunned! There they were, from floor to ceiling in this not-
very-large alcove, in poor light, THE QUEEN OF SHEBA, KING
SOLOMON, THE DREAM OF CONSTANTINE, THE VICTORY
OF HERACLIUS strong and beautiful, the color fresh and delectable.
The next morning in better light, I noticed the meter box where for a lira
or two one could buy a few minutes of light.

ASSISI:
From the farmland surrounding it, Assisi looks like the pink hill towns
in the backgrounds of Renaissance paintings. It is small, set upon its
west-facing hill with the church, monastery, and tomb of St. Francis at the
north end like a prow, and the church and tomb of St. Clare at the stern.
St. Clare's body lies on display in a grotto under her church. With the
help of my opera glasses, I could see the bones ofher face through a mask
of black wire mesh. The glasses were snatched from my hand by a barrel-
shaped woman in a flowered dress who then passed them on to a coven
of pungent nuns who looked and exclaimed, and grabbed the glasses from
each other, until I snatched them back and escaped up a narrow marble
stairway that led to another fearsome grotto, the original tomb of
St. Clare.
SIENA:
Drove into town through the massive Porte San Marco, looking for a
hotel we had chosen from our guidebook. The traffic carried us through
the one-way streets and dumped us outside the city walls where we had to
go around the city to get back to Porte San Marco. It took us several
circumnavigations before we found the hotel, which turned out to be a
hundred yards from the Porte San Marco. It was so dreadful that even
Herman-the-Intrepid quailed. So, we went out of town and found the
lovely old Villa Taraia on a hillside overlooking Siena.
In the evening we walked a mile down a white gravel road to a trattoria
for dinner. Sunday night madness was in progress... whole families, three
or four generations, were shovelling in mountains of delicious-looking
food that perfumed the air with the intoxicating scent of herbs and garlic.
We were starved. Our waiter (#2 son of the owner) took our order and
then went out to grab a ride on a friend's horse. The service left a lot to be
desired, but when the food finally arrived it was delicious and we wiped
our plates clean.
We walked back to our lodgings through starlit vineyards and filched a
few grapes for dessert, and so to bed tired and happy. The next morning
I tried to do a watercolor of Siena lying sienna-colored in the distance but
was disgusted with the result. Herman consoled me with ripe figs from an
abandoned farm nearby.
We picked up Maggie and Herb at the Siena Railroad station on their
return from Rome, scrunched into the car, and went to Gello.
Gello is an abandoned hill town with a small church, a priest, and a few
houses that can be rented from the owner, a Milan architect, who installed
electricity and plumbing but otherwise left the ancient farmhouses intact.
We drove past San Gimignano in the twilight, its towers looking like
the skyline of a metropolis. At the village of Pontegenori we found Beppa
(the caretaker of Gello), a skinny little woman in a shapeless black dress
who spoke no English. Our Italian was suitable for ordering "pasta con
vongole" and not much else. We bought bread, milk, salami and cheese at
the village shop. Beppa directed us up a winding gravel road overhung
with trees and brambles, past a few ancient hulks of buildings and through
a narrow archway into a courtyard where we parked and unloaded the
yellow lemon. We walked up a few stone steps into the whitewashed
kitchen with its crooked tree-trunk roofbeams, glowing old floor tiles, and
thick stone walls, found two little bathrooms in unexpected places and just
enough furniture and basic necessities.
The next day HH&M went off to Siena for the day and I had Gello to
myself (except for the Padre, who didn't surface, and a herd of black goats
that did). I took this opportunity to paint a watercolor of the kitchen and
cook some onions, peppers and tomatoes (from the farmer down the
road) for a pasta sauce. I showered and washed my hair in the funny little
stall at the top of the stairs next to the hot water heater. HH&M got back
around four, shlepping an enormous cockerel complete with toenails and
crested head, but for some mysterious reason the beak had been cut off.
We four ate that one bird, in several guises, for several days.
Thursday September 24. We folded ourselves into the Tin Lemon and
whizzed off over the spaghetti-looped roads to San Giminagno, a small
medieval city with some of the few remaining "sky-scraper" towers built
by wealthy merchants of the fourteenth century. The stark, plain
Romanesque cathedral abided by Schwartz's Law and contained
staggering treasures. Frescos by Taddeo di Bartolo (1396) covered every
wall. On the left Old Testament, on the right New Testament, in the
middle (in an arcade) Heaven and Hell. Hell, as usual, was the most
enchanting, with demons, fire, vicious beasts, and bodies writhing in
eternal damnation.

GREECE
My friend Linda Pearman asked me if I would conduct a painting
workshop in Spain or Portugal. "How about the Greek Islands?" I asked.
Linda said "OK, which one?" Crete... too big. Mykanos... too small.
Naxos... just right. We flew to Athens and took the ferry from Piraeus at
7:30a.m. and arrived at Naxos 4:30p.m. after a rainy but calm passage.
On board the ferry, the loud speakers ROARED - Beseme Mucho, Scott
Joplin rags, Bousouki renditions, Bob Dylan favorites, Arab laments,
Rolling Stones and Beatles medleys. BLASTING, BLARING,
BOOMING! People sleeping through it all on red and blue naugahyde
benches, ghostly islands slipping by in the mist, children crying, filthy
toilets overflowing, men, women and children sipping Jolly Cola through
thin straws. A woman in a ratty fur jacket, red stretchpants, white tennis
shoes, face covered by a black and gold brocade shawl, slept through the
whole trip. An ancient woman, dressed in black, blocked out the
cacophony with a yellow Sony Walkman. Two young Danish artists were
"Making ART" by stepping into paint and then stamping their running
shoes on large sheets of paper. A young man sang and played the
Bousouki in competition with the loud speaker. We read, sketched, ate
potato chips and drank Jolly Cola through thin straws.
At the Naxos pier we were met by "our host" Bobby, the English
husband of the darkly beautiful Despina who owned the hotel Ariadne.
Bobby's job consisted of perfecting his tan, wearing tight, white, skimpy
shorts over his cute little buns, staying out late, and driving Despina to
distraction. The one-story Hotel Ariadne faces the blue Aegean and is
situated near an exquisite little white-domed chapel which is next to a
graffiti-covered ruin with a rusted sun chaise on the sagging roof. To the
west is a small but majestic knoll of granite boulders surrounded by pads
of wildflowers: yellow and red poppies, pink mallows, purple stance,
white, yellow, and multicolored daisies, all dancing and bobbing in
the wind.
I gave a painting demonstration every morning, we painted on location
all day, and had an exhibition and critique with our Metaxa in the evening
before going out to dinner. After a bit of Metaxa, everything looked just
dandy. At the end of two weeks, I met Herman in Piraeus where we
rented a car and toured the Peleponnese peninsula and the island of
Xanthos in the Adriatic.

JAPAN
We traveled to Japan with a group of good friends who turned out to
be the jolliest of travel companions. Bill Wilson and Ed Smalle collect and
sell Japanese antiques and go to Japan frequently, so this was a unique
opportunity to go with them and our friends the Perloffs, Klausenstocks,
and Kirclmers.
Tokyo, Sunday May 7, 1983. Near Ueno Park, walked the narrow
streets lined with mini-houses decorated with front-step "gardens," i.e.,
plants in pots, jars, plastic pails or boxes, and basins of water containing
goldfish and waterlilies. Joined the gang for breakfast... eggs poached in
heart shapes, ham, orange juice, coffee and thick slabs of buttered toast.
Our hotel room in the Ogaisu Hotel is 9' by 10', and contains twin beds,
one arm chair and one straight chair, one small table, and a TV on a small
refrigerator stocked with beer, whiskey and snacks. Bathroom 4'x4' with
small deep tub, sink and western toilet... very nice.
Went downtown to get rail-passes and visit a department store. Ed and
Bill are showing us around, otherwise it would never occur to us to visit a
department store. Well! It is an experience! Every department store has a
food floor in the basement, an area the size of a city block crammed with
stands and counters of raw, roasted, broiled, boiled, smoked, pickled,
dried, marinated, stewed and fried chicken, beef, lamb, pork, game, fish,
sea food, salamis, hams, and sausages; fruits and vegetables from all over
the world (one apple $4); breads, cakes, pastries, cookies, candy; vats,
buckets, and barrels of pickled EVERYTHING; noodles, teas, coffees,
canned goods, cheeses, beans (fermented and in pastes) with free samples
of everything. We went crazy with new taste sensations.
The top floor of the store is a garden shop with every kind of wonder-
fully ingenious implement, exotic and homely plants, and a large pond
where kids can catch goldfish. (At the end of our trip Herman went back
and bought a rare Buddha's Belly bamboo which was quarantined and
murdered by Customs when we got home.) Had dinner arranged by
Midori, Ed and Bill's friend, in a small restaurant. Our gang of ten sat on
the floor with our backs to the wall, with a square table filling the center
of the room. We feasted on sashimi, fried crab balls, boiled beef (superb),
raw whelks (feh!), fish soup, raw squid, tofu custard, and rice soup, each
"course" served in a unique and beautiful dish.
The next morning I went out at 6a.m., sat on a curbstone and painted
a street scene until stiff with cold. After breakfast we took the fast, quiet,
spotlessShinkansen (Bullet Train) to Nagoya where we changed trains for
Takayama. I had a box lunch (Obento) consisting of rice balls, a tiny grilled
fish, a piece of chicken, pickles, and a perfect whole baby octopus in an
exquisite, paper-thin, compartmented wooden box.

TURKEY
We toured Turkey in 1985 in two rented cars with our friends the
Perloffs, Aulls, and Klausenstocks.
The first impression of Istanbul is of a rather sooty, crumbly, broken-
down city with every hill and vista decorated with fat domes and slender
minarets, no level land, and streets cobbled in fan patterns. I noticed the
cobble pattern because it is essential to look where you step... there are
no warning signs, just pitfalls. The street bazaars are a delight; you can
buy kitchenware, flowers, plants, live chickens, leeches, parrots, rolls of
designer labels (Dior, Calvin, Lacoste, Lauren), enormous copper cooking
caldrons, spices, nuts and dried fruit in big burlap bags, books, amulets,
jewelry, mountains of aluminum pots and pans, colorful flowered fabrics,
shoes, clothing, buttons, rick-rack, ribbons, cheap earrings by the cardfull,
sponges, and lucky charms.
Keith and Paula Aull came back from the market with a lucky charm
(two beaded birds on a beaded and fringed perch) that bus drivers and
"Taksi" drivers hang on their mirrors to ward off the evil eye. It seems to
work, because the style of driving is so crazy... no lanes, a total free-for-all.
It seems as though every car should have dented sides and fenders, but no,
each car slips through like a sardine in a merging school of fish. I bought
some too, since it couldn't hurt.
We rested at tea tables shaded by giant sycamore trees, on large island-
like plazas surrounded by rushing rivers of traffic. Tea "runners" carry little
swinging trays with small curved glasses of tea to tables, shops, bars,
offices, and the empty glasses, in their little saucers, are seen on doorsteps,
windowsills, sidewalks, and ledges waiting to be picked up on the tea
runner's return trip.
We ate in narrow storefront restaurants with window displays of baking
pans filled with savory-looking food: stuffed eggplants, peppers and
squashes, meat balls in brown sauce, white beans in paprika sauce, mutton
stew, bulgur pilaf, rice pilaf, cacik (pronouncedzhahzheek, cucumber and
yoghurt soup), kebabs, salads, and flaky-sticky desserts. The bread in
Turkey is sensational. Oh yes, we also saw Topkapi and many mosques.
The Turks seemed very friendly and honest. In Istanbul we were chased
by two women in full moslem tents who dragged us back to where we had
bought a coiled copper rug beater for fifty cents. The older woman said
we had been overcharged and she made the vendor refund some money.
Later Herman got a shoeshine, which turned into street theater.
Everyone sitting nearby got into the act. The same two women, who
seemed to have appointed themselves our guardian angels, came over and
admonished the poor, skinny, miserable, filthy shoeshiner not to

<!-- page 265 -->

overcharge us. But another shoeshine boy, who spoke some English, told
us the old man was crazy, slept in the dirt and didn't eat enough. The boy
then lent his "competitor" a polishing rag so he could do a good job.
People-watching is a sartorial time-trip. You see women wearing modern
dress, maxi-coats, voluminous flowered skirts, billowing flowered
bloomers, or black or brown tents (chador). The men wear dark suits
(sometimes with wide bloomers) and big black mustaches - occasionally
you see caftans and turbans.
After we left Istanbul we drove down the Aegean coast past ancient
Troy to the miracle of Ephesis, the charm of Kas, and turned inland at
Mersin toward Cappadoccia. In the small town of Nevsihir, we (Judy,
Oskar, Herman and I) were "kidnapped" by a high school girl who
beckoned and smiled until we followed her home to have tea with her
family. With smiles, gestures and a world atlas, we managed to
communicate that we wished each other well, took pictures of the family
and neighbors, and left with a glow of international good-feeling.
We returned the cars in Ankara and took the train back to Istanbul,
splurging on a first-class compartment for the overnight trip. A fat, kindly
porter in a soiled uniform showed us to our gloomy, dirty little compart-
ment which contained an upper and lower bunk, a drop-down sink and an
overpowering smell of urine. Our jolly attendant (who slept on the floor
in front of the ladies w.c.) brought us our breakfast on a tray. He placed
the tray on the seat between us, and with his grubby fingers picked up
chunks of feta cheese, olives and toast, slapped them onto small plates
which he placed on a narrow window ledge next to two little glasses of tea
and two lumps of sugar, all the while beaming like a fond father. We ate
our elegantly-served breakfast with good appetite. From Istanbul we took
a ferry to Prince's Island, off the coast of Asia Minor. It was "Youth Day"
and the ferry was seething and bubbling with holiday-makers dancing to
drums and tambourines. Men, women, and children, wiggling and shaking
to the irresistible beat, laughing and clapping, standing on the seats and in
between. At one end of the ship a group of break-dancers twitched, jerked
and twirled to a loud "ghetto-blaster" (they said they learned it from TV).
The island is lovely and quiet as no motor vehicles are allowed. Our last

INDIA
February 1986. We (and Judy and Oskar Klausenstock and Phil Perloff)
arrived in Delhi at 3a.m. The airport was cold and grey with no one inside
the building except the passengers from our flight (tight security). Near
the exit there was a large, dirty, fogged window with dark, blanket-
wrapped, anxious faces pressed against the glass looking like souls in
purgatory. From this miserable drabness we were driven by a turbanned
Sikh to the Taj Mahal Hotel, a veritable maharajah's palace with white
marble floors, carved balustrades, and luxurious shops stocked with
sumptuous silks and works of art.
Our room overlooked a sea of billowing green treetops toward the
domes and spires of the government buildings, with flocks of green
parrots flashing about. We were in an elegant neighborhood of broad
avenues, stately homes and embassies, but there were people living on
the streets, drying their laundry on the asphalt paving, defecating in the
gutters, and cattle roaming through the crazy traffic, bedding down in
busy traffic circles and grazing in the parks.
From our hotel room we looked down on a mini-farm, complete with
black buffalo and vegetable garden. Across the road, next to a taxi stand,
an outdoor eatery served hot food across a stone wall to the taxi drivers
who stood on stone slabs which spanned an open running sewer. Oh
wonderful, contrasty India!
It was not possible to do much on-site sketching, but I took a lot of
photographs and did a few studio paintings from them.

NEPAL
We flew to Katmandu after a long delay in the Varanesi (Benares)
airport. The next day we were driven to Chitawan where we slept in tents,
rode elephants into tall grass to look at rhinos, walked with a guide
through tiger territory (saw only footprints), and floated through moon-
stone morning mists and crocodile-infested waters in dugout canoes.
From Chitawan we were driven to Pokara at the foot of the Himalayas.
We got up at sunrise to see magnificent Annapurna covered with pink
snow. I sat on a stone slab in a hidden nook, flowers all around, a fish
eagle on a branch above, and tried to paint but my fingers got stiff with
cold and I went inside for hot porridge. When I went back to finish the
sketch I found all my gear covered with almost invisible pink ants.
Chocolate bar crumbs! Next a.m. tried another watercolor and was
besieged by camera-clicking Japanese tourists. Color and shadows
changing rapidly, fingers numb with cold, working fast as possible,
clouds moving in: oh, an artist's life! The appropriate conclusion to the
morning's work was a breakfast of weak Nescafe and a dab of slimy
scrambled eggs on wet toast. (The Fishtail Lodge is not world-famous for
it's haute cuisine.)

BOTANIZING IN KENYA
We were met at the Nairobi airport by tall, handsome Gilfrid Powys
whom we knew only through correspondence. He and his wife Patricia are
avid and knowledgeable botanizers, and had corresponded with Herman
about plant collecting and writing articles for the botanical books that he
publishes.
After a night in Nairobi, Gilfrid flew us (and Ron LaFon, plant wizard
and photographer) to his Rumuruti ranch in his four-seater Cherokee. We
flew over coffee and tea plantations, herds of elephants, and the river from
which the ranch gets its water. Patricia very kindly made us welcome,
though barely recovered from their daughter Anne's great wedding bash.

Herman and I spent a week touring the Game Parks and then Gilfrid
flew us to his Galana ranch which is west of Malindi (where his daughter
Anne and her new husband live). Galana is a vast tract of wild bush
country, complete with all the wild game found in the Game Parks plus
large commercial herds of cattle. The manager, Brian Heath, a modern
"cowboy," keeps an eye on things from a small plane. Brian, his wife Sue,
and their two young children live in a white stucco, tile-floored house with
spacious verandas on a little bluff overlooking the Galana river. We stayed
in a comfortable, thatched-roofed guest house with a large mosquito net
tent over the beds and a white bathroom with a huge bathtub. Our
bedroom opened onto a tile-floored veranda room with table and chairs
where Ron and Herman cataloged and labelled the newly collected plants
while I watched blue and orange lizards change color.
If the great American pastime is shopping, the great Kenyan pastime is
safari-ing (safari merely means "on the move"). The rover and truck are
loaded with food, drink, shovels and spare tires and off we go, driving for
hours through the bush, stopping now and then to change a tire or collect
a plant, detour around a mud wallow via a path cut through the bush by
Gilfrid and his powerfulpangs. The truck got stuck in little stream
between two steep banks with fresh lion tracks in the mud. While Gilfrid,
Herman, Anne and her husband, Ron, Sue and a friend worked on getting
the truck out, Lali, Sue's five-year-old daughter, played happily in the
stream. It took three hours to get the truck out. In the meantime, Anne lit
the charcoal and when it started to rain we moved the grill to a little but
near the pumping station. The rain didn't last long, so we soon had the
Kudu Kebabs ready and had a hilarious picnic of Kudu Kebabs, fruit,
salad, cheese, bread, pickles, chutney and beer sitting in the middle of a
dusty clearing near a pumping station, recalling all the idyllic picnic sites
we had passed before getting stuck.
Because we couldn't risk going back over the stream to take the short
way home (two hours) we had to go the long way (four hours). Suddenly,
as the sun was going down, the vehicles came to a halt... time for tea!
So there in the African twilight, with giraffes' necks silhouetted against
the sunset, we had our tea and then continued on through the pitch dark
night until we saw a pinpoint of light from the home airstrip.
On the way home from another safari at twilight, we detoured for
a look at one of Gilfrid's herds and were given hot, strong, sweet tea in
enamel mugs by the Somali cattlemen. The cattle are native African cattle
similar to Brahma but smaller and of many different colors. They have
been selectively bred (not mixed with other breeds) and are the most
beautiful cattle in the world: ivory, gold, cream, taupe or russet with
velvet skin hanging in luxurious folds down their throats, smoky eyes
and noses, and graceful horns. They created archaic friezes as they milled
about in the amber dust.
